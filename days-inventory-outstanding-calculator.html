What is Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO)?

Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO) — also called Days Sales of Inventory (DSI) — measures how many days, on average, your inventory sits in the warehouse before being sold. It is the inverse of Inventory Turnover. Lower DIO means faster cash conversion; higher DIO means capital is locked in stock longer.

DIO is one of the three components of the Cash Conversion Cycle: CCC = DIO + DSO − DPO. Reducing DIO directly reduces working capital requirements and improves cash flow — without needing more revenue or external financing.

Why This Calculator Matters

Cash Flow Impact

Every 10-day reduction in DIO on ₹1 Cr of annual COGS frees ₹27L of working capital. This directly reduces your bank OD requirement and saves interest cost.

Identify Overstock

A rising DIO signals growing overstock — you're buying more than you're selling. Catch it early via monthly DIO tracking before it becomes a dead stock problem.

Benchmark Your Sector

FMCG: 15–30 days. Fashion: 45–90 days. Electronics: 30–60 days. Industrial: 60–120 days. If your DIO is above benchmark, your working capital is less efficient than competitors.

Input to CCC

DIO feeds directly into the Cash Conversion Cycle. Improving DIO is the most actionable lever for most product businesses since it is entirely within your control — unlike DSO (customer payment terms) or DPO (supplier terms).

Frequently Asked Questions
Is lower DIO always better?
Generally yes, but not always. Too low a DIO can mean you are running out of stock and missing sales (stockouts). The optimal DIO balances working capital efficiency with service level. Use the Safety Stock and Reorder Point calculators alongside DIO to find your optimal inventory level.
What is the difference between DIO and Days Sales of Inventory (DSI)?
They are essentially the same metric with different names. DIO uses COGS in the denominator (more accurate for inventory valuation). DSI sometimes uses Revenue instead of COGS. Always use COGS for the most accurate comparison — using Revenue overstates inventory efficiency by the gross margin percentage.
How do I reduce DIO?
Five levers: (1) Improve demand forecasting to avoid over-ordering, (2) Run ABC analysis and reduce safety stock on C-class slow-movers, (3) Negotiate more frequent, smaller deliveries using EOQ, (4) Identify and liquidate dead stock — it dramatically inflates DIO, (5) Use FIFO picking to prevent older stock sitting at the back of the warehouse.
How often should I calculate DIO?
Monthly for most businesses. Weekly for fast-moving categories (FMCG, Q-commerce suppliers). Quarterly for slow-moving, high-value items (industrial, capital goods). Track DIO as a trend — a rising DIO over 3 months is a strong signal of inventory buildup that needs attention.
