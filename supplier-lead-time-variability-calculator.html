📐 Lead Time Variability Formula
Avg Lead Time = Sum of Observations ÷ Count
Std Dev (σ) = √( Σ(LT − Avg)² ÷ (n−1) )
Safety Stock (95% SL) = 1.645 × σ_LT × Avg Daily Demand
Coefficient of Variation = σ ÷ Avg Lead Time × 100%
What is Lead Time Variability?

Lead time variability is the inconsistency in how long your supplier takes to deliver. If a supplier's average lead time is 21 days but sometimes delivers in 14 days and sometimes takes 35 days, that variability forces you to hold significantly more safety stock than a supplier who reliably delivers in exactly 21 days every time.

This is one of the most underappreciated costs of supplier unreliability. The financial cost of variability shows up not in stockouts (which you prevent with extra safety stock) but in the carrying cost of that extra buffer stock — which you are holding purely because your supplier is unpredictable. Reducing supplier lead time variability is often more valuable than reducing average lead time.

Frequently Asked Questions
How many lead time observations do I need?
Minimum 5 for a rough estimate, 10–15 for reasonable accuracy, 20+ for statistical confidence. Use the last 6–12 months of purchase order data from your ERP or supplier confirmation emails. Include outliers (port delays, supplier shutdowns) — they are part of the real variability your safety stock must cover.
What is a good Coefficient of Variation (CV) for lead time?
CV = Standard Deviation ÷ Average Lead Time × 100%. CV below 10%: excellent supplier reliability. 10–20%: acceptable. 20–30%: high variability — negotiate SLA improvements. Above 30%: critically unreliable — dual-source this supplier immediately.
How do I reduce lead time variability?
Five approaches: (1) Share rolling 3-month forecasts with supplier so they can pre-position materials, (2) Negotiate production slot booking 4–6 weeks ahead, (3) Switch from sea to air for time-critical replenishments, (4) Qualify a second supplier as a backup for same SKUs, (5) Build buffer stock at the supplier's end under a VMI (Vendor Managed Inventory) arrangement.
Is lead time variability more important than average lead time?
Often yes. Reducing average lead time from 21 to 18 days saves 3 days × demand in safety stock. But reducing variability from σ=5 to σ=2 days reduces safety stock by (5−2) × demand × Z-score — often a larger saving. And reliability improvements compound because you can reduce safety stock without increasing stockout risk.
