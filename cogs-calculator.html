📐 COGS Formula
COGS = Beginning Inventory + Purchases − Ending Inventory
Gross Profit = Revenue − COGS
Gross Margin % = (Gross Profit ÷ Revenue) × 100
What is the COGS Calculator?

Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) is the total direct cost of products sold during a period. It includes what you paid to buy or produce the goods — product cost, inbound freight, customs duties, and direct manufacturing costs. It does NOT include rent, marketing, salaries of office staff, or other operating expenses.

COGS is the most important line on your P&L after revenue. Gross Margin = Revenue − COGS. Every pricing, procurement, and product mix decision should be grounded in accurate COGS. Indian D2C brands frequently miscalculate COGS by missing inbound freight, GST on purchases (non-recoverable portion), and customs duties.

A Flipkart seller calculates COGS for Q3 (July–September):

Why COGS Matters for Your Business

Accurate Pricing

If you price at a 30% markup over purchase price but haven't included freight and GST in COGS, you're selling at a lower margin than you think — or even at a loss.

GST Filing

COGS feeds directly into your GSTR-3B. Accurate COGS ensures correct ITC claims and avoids scrutiny during GST audits. Non-recoverable GST on purchases increases your true COGS.

Investor & Bank Reporting

Banks, VCs, and PE investors assess gross margin as the primary measure of business health. Accurate COGS is non-negotiable for any funding or credit facility.

Inventory Valuation

COGS determines ending inventory value on your balance sheet. Wrong COGS = wrong inventory value = misleading financial statements. Especially critical at year-end for tax computation.

Frequently Asked Questions
Should I include GST in COGS?
If you are a GST-registered business and can claim Input Tax Credit (ITC), do NOT include the recoverable GST in COGS — it nets to zero. However, GST that you cannot recover (e.g. RCM on GTA freight where you cannot claim ITC, or purchases used for exempt supplies) should be included in COGS as it is a real cost to your business.
Should inbound freight be included in COGS?
Yes. Freight paid to receive inventory from your supplier (freight-in) is a direct product cost and should be included in COGS. This is different from outbound freight (shipping to customers), which is a selling expense. Indian businesses frequently exclude freight-in from COGS, causing them to overstate gross margins.
What is a good gross margin for Indian e-commerce?
It varies significantly by category. FMCG: 25–40%. Beauty & personal care: 40–60%. Fashion & apparel: 45–65%. Electronics: 10–20%. D2C brands: 45–65% (needs to cover high marketing spend). If your gross margin is below 30%, it is very difficult to build a profitable business once you include marketing, logistics, and operating costs.
What is the difference between COGS and operating expenses?
COGS includes only direct costs of producing or buying your products — raw materials, direct labour, inbound freight, customs. Operating expenses (OPEX) are costs of running the business — warehouse rent, marketing, salaries of admin staff, software subscriptions. COGS comes before gross profit; OPEX comes after. Mixing the two gives you an inaccurate gross margin.
