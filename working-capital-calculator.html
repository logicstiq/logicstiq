What is Working Capital Calculator?

Working capital in a supply chain context is the amount of cash tied up in your inventory, receivables, and payables at any given time. High working capital means more cash is locked in operations and less is available for investment, debt repayment, or growth.

For Indian SMEs, working capital management is often the difference between a profitable and a struggling business. A company generating ₹5 Cr in annual revenue might have ₹1.5 Cr permanently tied up in inventory and receivables — money that could otherwise reduce bank loans, fund new product development, or simply stay as cash buffer.

The Working Capital Formula

📐 Formula
Working Capital = Inventory + Accounts Receivable − Accounts Payable

Working capital is the cash your business needs to fund day-to-day operations. In supply chain, it is primarily driven by how long cash is locked in inventory and how quickly customers pay versus how quickly you pay suppliers. Reducing working capital frees up cash for growth without requiring external funding.

A distributor in Pune has the following supply chain financials:

Working Capital₹18L + ₹12L − ₹6L

This distributor has ₹24 lakh permanently tied up in working capital. If they could reduce inventory by 20% and negotiate 15-day shorter receivables, they could free up ₹5-6 lakh of cash — equivalent to a 6-month EMI reduction on a ₹50L business loan.

Why Working Capital Matters for Your Business

Working capital efficiency is one of the most overlooked drivers of profitability for Indian businesses. Most focus on revenue and gross margin — but the cash conversion cycle determines how much of that profit actually becomes available cash.

  • Bank loan requirement: Lower working capital means lower working capital loans and lower interest burden
  • Inventory optimisation: Reducing excess inventory by 20% has a direct, immediate cash impact
  • Supplier negotiation: Longer payable terms reduce working capital without reducing inventory levels
  • Investor metric: VCs and investors assess working capital efficiency as a sign of operational maturity

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a good working capital ratio for an Indian SME?▾
A current ratio (current assets ÷ current liabilities) of 1.5 to 2.0 is generally considered healthy for Indian SMEs. This means for every ₹1 of short-term liabilities, you have ₹1.5 to ₹2 of current assets. Too low means liquidity risk. Too high means excess cash is tied up unproductively. For supply chain heavy businesses, aim to keep Days Sales of Inventory (DSI) below 45 days.
How does inventory management reduce working capital?▾
Every rupee of excess inventory is a rupee of working capital. Reducing inventory through better demand forecasting, smaller more frequent orders (using EOQ), and eliminating dead stock directly reduces working capital requirements. A company that reduces inventory from ₹20L to ₹15L has freed ₹5L of cash — which can reduce an overdraft facility or fund growth.
What is the Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC)?▾
The Cash Conversion Cycle measures how many days it takes to convert inventory investment into cash from sales. CCC = Days Inventory Outstanding + Days Sales Outstanding − Days Payable Outstanding. A lower CCC means faster cash recovery. Negative CCC (achieved by companies like Amazon and Zara) means customers pay before you pay your suppliers — the ideal working capital position.
