What is ABC analysis?

ABC analysis is an inventory classification technique that divides your SKUs into three categories — A, B, and C — based on their contribution to total revenue or inventory value. It is based on the Pareto Principle (the 80/20 rule): approximately 20% of your SKUs typically generate 80% of your revenue.

ABC analysis answers a fundamental question every supply chain professional faces: you can't manage all 500 SKUs with the same level of attention — so which ones deserve the most focus?

🎯
The core insight: Not all inventory is equal. Treating a ₹2L/month SKU and a ₹200/month SKU with the same reorder process, same safety stock policy, and same management attention is inefficient. ABC analysis gives you a systematic way to differentiate your approach — and focus your energy where it generates the most value.

A, B, C categories explained

A
~20% of SKUs
~70–80% of revenue
Your highest-value items. Tight control, high safety stock, frequent review, premium supplier relationships.
B
~30% of SKUs
~15–20% of revenue
Mid-value items. Standard processes apply. Review monthly. Moderate safety stock levels.
C
~50% of SKUs
~5–10% of revenue
Low-value items. Simplified ordering. Minimal safety stock. Consider rationalisation or discontinuation.
💡
The percentages are guidelines, not rules. In some businesses, A items are the top 10% of SKUs generating 75% of revenue. In others it is 25% generating 70%. The exact split depends on your product mix. What matters is the principle: concentrate management resources on the items that drive most value.

How to conduct ABC analysis — step by step

  1. List all your SKUs with their annual revenue or annual usage value (unit cost × units consumed)
  2. Sort descending by annual revenue — highest value SKU at the top
  3. Calculate cumulative revenue — running total as a percentage of total revenue
  4. Classify: SKUs in the top 0–70% of cumulative revenue = A. Next 70–90% = B. Remaining 90–100% = C
  5. Review and adjust — some SKUs may be strategically important regardless of current revenue (e.g. a new product launch, a strategic customer's key item)
  6. Assign management policies by category — different service levels, safety stock, reorder policies

Inventory strategy by category

A
Class A — Maximum Attention
Service level 98–99%. High safety stock (3–4 weeks). Weekly or even daily stock reviews. Tight supplier SLAs with penalty clauses. Multiple supplier options to reduce single-source risk. Demand forecast updated weekly. These items cannot go out of stock — the revenue impact is too high.
B
Class B — Standard Process
Service level 95%. Moderate safety stock (2 weeks). Monthly stock reviews. Standard supplier terms. Demand forecast updated monthly. Apply EOQ-based ordering. These items need solid management but not the same intensity as A-items.
C
Class C — Simplified & Lean
Service level 90%. Minimal safety stock. Quarterly reviews. Simple two-bin or visual reorder system. Consider rationalising this range — is it worth the complexity for 12% of revenue? If keeping, automate reorders to reduce management time. Review for dead stock candidates.

ABC-XYZ analysis — the advanced version

ABC analysis classifies by value. XYZ analysis classifies by demand variability:

  • X items: Stable, predictable demand — easy to forecast and manage
  • Y items: Seasonal or trend-driven demand — moderate variability
  • Z items: Highly erratic, unpredictable demand — difficult to forecast

Combining the two creates a 9-cell matrix. The most important combination is AZ items — high value but highly unpredictable. These require the most sophisticated demand planning: higher safety stock, more frequent forecasting, and direct communication with customers about upcoming demand spikes.

The easiest to manage are CX items — low value and completely predictable. These are candidates for full automation — set a reorder point and forget about them.

✅
Start simple: If you have not done ABC analysis before, start with just A, B, C based on revenue. Once you have that working consistently, add XYZ for demand variability. Trying to do ABC-XYZ as a first analysis is unnecessarily complex and often leads to analysis paralysis.

Common ABC Analysis Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

Mistake 1 — Classifying by units sold, not value

The most common ABC analysis error is sorting by volume (units sold) instead of value (revenue or COGS contribution). A product that sells 10,000 units at ₹5 each contributes ₹50,000. A product that sells 100 units at ₹8,000 each contributes ₹8,00,000. Volume-based classification would put the first product in Category A and the second in Category C — exactly backwards from a value management perspective. Always classify by annual revenue contribution or annual consumption value, not by units.

Mistake 2 — Never updating the classification

ABC classifications are not permanent. A new product launch might start as a C-item and become an A-item within 6 months. A seasonal product might be A in Q4 and C in Q1. A trend-driven SKU might move from A to dead stock in 90 days. Review and update your ABC classification at minimum quarterly, and for fast-moving categories like fashion and electronics, monthly. Using 12-month-old ABC classifications to make today's inventory decisions is a common source of misallocated management attention and capital.

Mistake 3 — Applying the same policy to all items within a class

All A-items are not equal. The top A-item (your #1 revenue SKU) needs more attention than the A-item at the bottom of the A boundary. Many companies improve on basic ABC by implementing tiered service levels within each class — A+ for the top 5% of revenue SKUs, A for the next 15%, and so on. This provides finer-grained management control for your highest-value products.

Mistake 4 — Ignoring strategic importance in classification

ABC analysis is purely quantitative — it tells you what generated value historically. It does not account for strategic importance. A new product in test phase might currently be a C-item but represents a critical future revenue stream. A product used only by your single largest customer might be a C-item by revenue but its absence would lose your biggest account. Always overlay strategic judgment on top of the quantitative ABC classification. Mark strategically important items for high service levels regardless of their current revenue rank.

Mistake 5 — Applying ABC to your entire catalogue at once the first time

If you have 2,000 SKUs and have never done ABC analysis before, starting with all 2,000 simultaneously creates an overwhelming data project. Instead, start with your top 200 SKUs by revenue — these almost certainly contain all your A-items and most of your B-items. Get the system working for these first, then extend to the full catalogue once the process is established and your team understands how to act on the classification.

ABC Analysis and Demand Forecasting

ABC classification directly determines how much forecasting sophistication each SKU deserves. A-items warrant statistical forecasting methods, frequent review cycles (weekly or daily for fastest movers), and dedicated attention to demand signals. B-items can use simpler moving average forecasts reviewed monthly. C-items can often use simple min/max reorder rules — when stock drops below a minimum level, order up to a maximum. This tiered forecasting approach — sophisticated for A, standard for B, automated for C — is where supply chain teams create the most efficiency. Every hour spent forecasting a C-item is an hour not spent managing an A-item that actually matters.
