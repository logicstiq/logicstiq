What is EOQ and why does it matter?

Every time you place a purchase order, you face the same question: how much should I order? Order too little and you're constantly reordering, paying high per-unit shipping costs, and risking stockouts. Order too much and you're tying up cash in inventory, paying storage fees, and risking dead stock.

The Economic Order Quantity (EOQ) is the mathematically optimal order quantity that minimises your total inventory costs — the exact point where your ordering costs and holding costs balance out perfectly.

💡
In plain English: EOQ tells you the single number — how many units to order at one time — that costs your business the least money overall. Not per unit. Total cost.

Developed by Ford W. Harris in 1913 and still one of the most used formulas in supply chain management, EOQ is relevant for anyone managing inventory — from a Tally-based distributor in Delhi to an Amazon FBA seller in Bangalore to a global FMCG company running SAP.

The two costs EOQ balances

EOQ works by finding the order quantity where two opposing costs are equal:

  • Ordering costs — costs you incur each time you place an order. Includes supplier communication time, purchase order processing, inbound freight, quality inspection, and receiving labour. These go DOWN as you order larger quantities (fewer orders per year).
  • Holding costs — costs of keeping inventory in your warehouse. Includes storage rent, insurance, capital tied up (opportunity cost), risk of obsolescence and damage. These go UP as you order larger quantities (more stock sitting).

The EOQ is the order quantity where these two costs intersect — your total annual inventory cost is at its lowest point.

The EOQ formula — explained simply

📐 The EOQ Formula
EOQ = √( 2 × D × S / H )
D = Annual demand (units/year) S = Ordering cost per order (₹) H = Holding cost per unit per year (₹) √ = Square root

Understanding each variable

D — Annual Demand

Total units you expect to sell or use in a year. If you sell 200 units per month, your annual demand is 2,400. Use historical sales data from your ERP, marketplace reports, or Tally stock summary. If you're new, estimate conservatively — you can always adjust.

S — Ordering Cost per Order

The total cost each time you place ONE purchase order. This includes the time spent communicating with the supplier (value your hour), purchase order paperwork, any fixed freight charges per shipment (not per unit), quality inspection time, and receiving/putaway labour. A typical ordering cost for an SME in India ranges from ₹200 to ₹2,000 per order depending on complexity.

H — Holding Cost per Unit per Year

What it costs to store one unit for one full year. Calculate as: (your average unit cost) × (annual holding cost rate). The holding cost rate is typically 20–30% of unit value for most businesses. It includes warehouse space (₹ per sq ft × space used), insurance, risk of damage/obsolescence, and opportunity cost of the capital tied up.

EOQ for Amazon FBA & marketplace sellers

EOQ works for marketplace sellers, but you need to adjust for two key differences from traditional retail:

1. Amazon FBA storage fees change your holding cost

Amazon charges monthly storage fees (₹75–₹2,250 per cubic metre depending on size and season). Add these to your holding cost calculation. During Q4 (October–December), Amazon's long-term storage fees spike — EOQ quantities should be lower during this period to avoid excess inventory charges.

2. Account for your lead time and reorder point together

EOQ tells you how much to order. Your Reorder Point (ROP) tells you when to order. Use them together:

  • Calculate EOQ first (how much)
  • Calculate your daily sales velocity (average daily units sold)
  • Multiply by lead time in days — that's your reorder point
  • Add safety stock for variability
⚠️
FBA Tip: For fast-moving SKUs (>50 units/day), standard EOQ often gives quantities too small for sea freight efficiency. In this case, apply a minimum order constraint equal to one full shipping container worth of product, and use EOQ as a guide rather than a hard rule.

Flipkart & Meesho sellers

For Flipkart Smart Fulfilment or Meesho Fulfilment, apply the same logic but substitute the platform's storage cost for H. Flipkart's storage charges are typically lower than Amazon's, which means your EOQ will be slightly higher (you can afford to hold more inventory).

5 common EOQ mistakes to avoid

1. Using the wrong demand period

EOQ requires annual demand. Many sellers accidentally use monthly demand. If your product sells 300 units/month, your D = 3,600 (not 300). Using 300 will produce an EOQ that's dramatically too small.

2. Ignoring seasonality

EOQ assumes constant demand, but most products have seasonal spikes (Diwali, summer, back-to-school). Calculate separate EOQs for peak season and off-peak season, or use a weighted annual demand that accounts for the spikes.

3. Underestimating the ordering cost

Most people only count freight. But ordering cost includes: your time or staff time to process the order (value this at your hourly rate), quality inspection, supplier coordination, any fixed fees charged by the supplier, and receiving/putaway labour. Underestimating S makes your EOQ too small.

4. Forgetting opportunity cost in holding cost

If you tie up ₹10 lakh in inventory, that ₹10 lakh isn't earning you anything. The opportunity cost — what you could have earned investing that money — is typically 10–15% per year and must be included in H. Many businesses only count warehouse rent and miss this completely.

5. Applying EOQ to all SKUs equally

EOQ is most valuable for your A-category SKUs (top 20% that drive 80% of revenue). For C-category SKUs (slow movers, low value), the calculation cost isn't worth the precision. Use ABC analysis first, then apply EOQ rigorously to your A items.

Limitations of EOQ

EOQ is a powerful starting point but it has assumptions that don't always hold in real life:

  • Assumes constant demand — real demand fluctuates. EOQ works best for stable, predictable SKUs.
  • Assumes constant lead time — if your supplier's lead time varies, your optimal order point changes. Pair EOQ with safety stock to handle this.
  • Ignores quantity discounts — if your supplier offers 10% off for orders over 500 units, pure EOQ may miss the savings. Compare EOQ cost vs. discounted bulk order cost separately.
  • Assumes no stockouts — EOQ minimises inventory cost but doesn't protect against stockouts. Always add safety stock on top of your EOQ-based ordering plan.
  • Ignores supplier MOQ — if your supplier's minimum order quantity is 200 units but your EOQ is 127, your effective EOQ becomes 200. Always check MOQ constraints.
💡
Bottom line: EOQ is the foundation of inventory optimisation — not the complete picture. Use it alongside Safety Stock, Reorder Point, and ABC Analysis for a complete inventory management system.
