What is Landed Cost?

Landed cost is the total cost of getting a product from your supplier's factory door to your warehouse shelf — not just what you paid the supplier. It includes the product price, international freight, insurance, customs duty, IGST, port and clearing charges, and domestic transport.

Most new importers and marketplace sellers price their products using only the supplier invoice value. That is a mistake. For a typical import into India, the true landed cost is often 25–45% higher than the supplier's price. If you set your selling price off the invoice value, that gap comes straight out of your margin — and sometimes turns a "profitable" SKU into a loss-maker.

💡
One-line definition: Landed cost = everything you spend, per unit, until the goods are sitting in your warehouse ready to sell.

Why Landed Cost Matters for Indian Importers & Sellers

If you sell on Amazon.in, Flipkart, Meesho, or through your own D2C store, three critical numbers depend on landed cost:

  • Selling price & margin — your gross margin is Selling Price minus Landed Cost (not minus invoice price). Get landed cost wrong and every margin report you run afterwards is wrong.
  • Break-even and ad spend — your maximum viable ACOS/ROAS on marketplace ads is a direct function of true landed cost.
  • Inventory valuation — GST and income-tax reporting require inventory valued at cost, which includes duties and freight, not just the supplier invoice.

Landed cost also lets you compare suppliers fairly. A supplier in Vietnam quoting $2.10 per unit may actually be cheaper than one in China quoting $1.95, once freight rates, duty structure under trade agreements, and lead-time-driven inventory costs are included.

The Landed Cost Formula

Landed Cost Formula
Landed Cost = Product Cost + Freight + Insurance + Customs Duty + Taxes + Clearing & Handling + Domestic Transport

And on a per-unit basis, which is what you actually use for pricing:

Landed Cost Per Unit
Landed Cost Per Unit = Total Landed Cost of the Shipment ÷ Number of Sellable Units
⚠️
Note the word "sellable". If 2% of your units arrive damaged or fail QC, divide by the good units only. Damaged stock inflates the real cost of every unit you can actually sell.

Every Cost Component Explained (India-Specific)

1. Product cost (FOB / EXW value)

The supplier invoice value. Check your Incoterm: EXW (Ex-Works) means you pay everything from the factory gate; FOB (Free on Board) means the supplier covers local transport and export clearance up to the origin port.

2. International freight

Sea freight (LCL or FCL) or air freight to the Indian port/airport. For customs purposes in India, freight forms part of the CIF value on which duty is assessed.

3. Insurance

Marine/cargo insurance, typically 0.3–0.5% of cargo value. If actual insurance is not declared, Indian customs adds a notional 1.125% of FOB for valuation.

4. Basic Customs Duty (BCD)

Charged on the assessable value (broadly, the CIF value). The rate depends on the HSN code of your product — commonly 10–20% for consumer goods, but always verify the current rate for your specific HSN code on ICEGATE before ordering.

5. Social Welfare Surcharge (SWS)

10% of the BCD amount (not of the product value). Small, but it compounds the duty stack.

6. IGST on imports

IGST is charged on (Assessable Value + BCD + SWS) at the GST rate applicable to your product (5%, 12%, 18% or 28%). Important: if you are GST-registered, IGST paid at import is usually available as input tax credit — so for margin math many sellers treat it as recoverable, while BCD and SWS are true costs. Confirm treatment with your CA.

7. Port, CHA and clearing charges

Customs House Agent (CHA) fees, terminal handling, container freight station charges, documentation, and any demurrage if clearance is delayed. For small LCL shipments these fixed charges can be a surprisingly large share of landed cost.

8. Domestic transport & last-mile to warehouse

Trucking from the port to your warehouse or to an FBA/Flipkart fulfilment centre, plus loading/unloading labour.

Worked Example — Importing from China to India

Say you import 1,000 units of a kitchen gadget at $2.00 FOB Shenzhen (₹84 per unit at ₹84/USD), HSN duty rate 20% BCD, GST rate 18%.

📦 Landed Cost Build-Up (1,000 units)
Product cost (FOB) — 1,000 × ₹84₹84,000
Sea freight (LCL, Shenzhen → Nhava Sheva)₹18,000
Insurance (0.5%)₹420
Assessable value (≈ CIF)₹1,02,420
Basic Customs Duty @ 20%₹20,484
Social Welfare Surcharge @ 10% of BCD₹2,048
IGST @ 18% on (AV + BCD + SWS) = ₹1,24,952₹22,491*
CHA + port + clearing charges₹9,500
Domestic transport to warehouse₹4,500
Total landed cost (excluding recoverable IGST)₹1,38,952
Landed cost per unit₹138.95

*IGST of ₹22,491 is excluded from the total above assuming you claim it as input tax credit. If you are not GST-registered, add it back — landed cost becomes ₹1,61,443, or ₹161.44 per unit.

The headline: a "₹84 product" actually costs ₹139–₹161 landed — 65–92% above invoice value in this small-shipment scenario. This is exactly the gap that quietly kills margins.

5 Costly Mistakes Sellers Make

  1. Pricing off the supplier invoice. Always price off landed cost per sellable unit.
  2. Wrong HSN classification. A wrong code can mean overpaying duty for years — or penalties for underpaying. Verify before your first shipment.
  3. Ignoring fixed charges on small shipments. CHA, documentation and port charges barely change with shipment size — small orders carry a heavy per-unit burden.
  4. Forgetting exchange-rate movement. Customs uses notified exchange rates; your bank uses another. A 3% INR swing between order and payment directly moves your landed cost.
  5. Counting damaged units as sellable. Divide total cost by good units only, and budget a QC/damage allowance of 1–3%.
