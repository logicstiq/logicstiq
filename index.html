What is inventory turnover ratio?

The inventory turnover ratio measures how many times a company sells and replaces its entire inventory stock within a given period — typically one year. It is one of the most fundamental metrics in supply chain and financial analysis because it reveals how efficiently a business converts its inventory investment into revenue.

🔄
Simple analogy: If a clothing store has ₹10 lakh of inventory and sells ₹50 lakh worth of goods in a year, their inventory turned over 5 times. That means they cycled through their entire stock 5 times. A higher number generally means better efficiency — less cash tied up in unsold goods.

Why inventory turnover matters

  • Cash flow: Slow-moving inventory ties up working capital. A business with ₹1 Cr of inventory turning 2x vs 6x per year has a ₹66L difference in capital efficiency
  • Warehouse costs: Inventory that sits in your warehouse costs money every day — rent, insurance, risk of damage and obsolescence
  • Freshness and relevance: In fashion, electronics, and FMCG, slow inventory turnover means products become outdated before they are sold
  • Credit terms: Banks and investors use inventory turnover to assess the health of your business. Low turnover raises red flags about demand and management quality

The formula — two versions explained

📐 Version 1 — Using Cost of Goods Sold (Most Accurate)
Inventory Turnover = Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) ÷ Average Inventory
📐 Version 2 — Using Net Sales Revenue
Inventory Turnover = Net Sales Revenue ÷ Average Inventory

Both are correct — but they produce different numbers. Version 1 (COGS) is preferred because it compares inventory at cost value to sales at cost value. Using revenue inflates the ratio by including your profit margin.

📐 Average Inventory
Average Inventory = (Opening Stock + Closing Stock) ÷ 2
📌
Which version does your ERP use? Most Indian ERPs like Tally use the Sales/Average Inventory version. SAP and NetSuite typically offer both. Check which version you are calculating to ensure your benchmarking comparisons are apples-to-apples.

The difference: COGS method gives 6x, revenue method gives 12x — for the same business. This is why you must know which method you are using before comparing to industry benchmarks. Most published benchmarks use the COGS method.

Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO) — the inverse metric

DIO converts inventory turnover into a more intuitive measure: how many days of stock do you have on hand?

📐 Days Inventory Outstanding
DIO = 365 ÷ Inventory Turnover Ratio

For our skincare brand: DIO = 365 ÷ 6.0 = 61 days. This means they are holding on average 61 days of inventory. Is that good? It depends on their industry and lead times — which brings us to benchmarks.

How to improve your inventory turnover

If your turnover is too low (too much stock)

  • Reduce reorder quantities: Use EOQ (Economic Order Quantity) to right-size orders instead of bulk buying for volume discounts that exceed your actual demand
  • Identify and liquidate dead stock: Any SKU with zero sales in 90+ days is a dead stock candidate. Offer discounts, bundle with fast-movers, or liquidate through secondary channels
  • Improve demand forecasting: Overstock almost always originates from over-forecasting. More accurate demand planning directly reduces excess inventory build-up
  • Reduce lead times: Shorter supplier lead times allow you to carry less safety stock and order more frequently in smaller quantities
  • ABC analysis: Focus most of your inventory management attention on A-category items (top 20% by revenue). C-category items often have disproportionately low turnover

If your turnover is too high (risk of stockouts)

  • Add safety stock: Very high turnover with frequent stockouts is not an asset — it means you are undersupplied. Calculate your safety stock level and maintain it
  • Increase reorder frequency: If lead times allow it, order more frequently in smaller quantities to maintain availability without holding excess stock
  • Strengthen supplier relationships: Negotiate faster replenishment windows with key suppliers to enable leaner inventory holding without stockout risk

Why Inventory Turnover Directly Impacts Your Cash Flow

Most supply chain professionals understand inventory turnover as a performance metric. What fewer appreciate is its direct mechanical link to working capital — the cash your business needs to operate day to day.

The working capital equation

Every unit of inventory sitting in your warehouse represents cash that is locked up and unavailable for anything else. Whether you borrowed money to fund that inventory (paying interest) or used your own capital (losing the opportunity to invest it elsewhere), idle inventory has a real financial cost that shows up nowhere in your P&L until you liquidate it.

The formula is direct: Working Capital Tied in Inventory = (Average Inventory Value) = (Annual COGS ÷ Inventory Turnover Ratio). A skincare brand with ₹1.2 Cr annual COGS running at 4x turnover holds ₹30 lakh in inventory on average. If they improve turnover to 8x, average inventory drops to ₹15 lakh — releasing ₹15 lakh of cash for marketing, product development, or debt repayment. That is the power of turnover improvement expressed in rupees, not ratios.

Inventory turnover and your cash conversion cycle

The Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC) measures how long it takes for a business to convert its inventory investment back into cash. It is calculated as: CCC = DIO + DSO − DPO (Days Inventory Outstanding + Days Sales Outstanding − Days Payable Outstanding).

Reducing DIO — by improving inventory turnover — directly shortens your CCC. For a D2C brand with a 90-day CCC, improving inventory turnover from 4x to 6x reduces DIO from 91 days to 61 days — shrinking the cash conversion cycle by 30 days. For a business doing ₹5 Cr annual revenue, that 30-day improvement frees approximately ₹41L of cash. This is why investors and banks look at inventory turnover — it reveals how efficiently a management team converts capital into revenue and back into cash.

Amazon and Flipkart FBA implications

For marketplace sellers, inventory turnover has a second dimension: platform storage fees. Amazon FBA charges monthly storage fees that escalate significantly after 365 days. Products with low inventory turnover accumulate these fees, eating directly into margins. A product turning over 3x per year and sitting in FBA for 120+ days before selling is paying approximately ₹2–8 per cubic foot per month in storage fees on top of its normal holding cost. This makes improving turnover for slow-moving marketplace inventory doubly urgent — you pay the opportunity cost of tied-up capital AND the platform's storage penalty simultaneously.

Seasonal businesses and turnover measurement

For businesses with strong seasonality — Diwali for electronics, summer for ACs, wedding season for fashion — annual inventory turnover ratios can be misleading. A business that sells 80% of its revenue in 4 months may show a healthy annual turnover of 6x, while actually sitting on 8–9 months of idle inventory during the off-season. In these cases, measure turnover by season or quarter rather than annually. A quarterly turnover below 1.5x for your off-peak period is a signal that your pre-season buying was too aggressive.
