What is Supply Chain Lead Time Calculator?

Supply chain lead time is the total elapsed time from placing a purchase order to having inventory available and ready to fulfil customer orders. It is the most critical input in reorder point calculation — underestimate it and you run out of stock before replenishment arrives.

For Indian importers, lead time variability (the difference between best-case and worst-case lead time) is often more important than average lead time. A product with an average 21-day lead time that ranges from 14 to 35 days requires much higher safety stock than one with a consistent 21-day lead time.

The Lead Time Formula

📐 Formula
Total Lead Time = Supplier Processing + Production + Transit + Customs + Receiving + Buffer

Lead time is not just transit time — it includes every step from when you place a purchase order to when stock is available for sale. Most businesses underestimate lead time by only counting transit time and ignoring supplier processing, customs delays, and warehouse receiving time.

Worked Example

A Delhi importer sources electronics from a manufacturer in China. Their typical lead time components:

📊 Example Calculation
Supplier confirms order2 days
Production time7 days
Sea freight (Shanghai to Mumbai)18 days
Customs clearance4 days
Inland freight (Mumbai to Delhi)2 days
Warehouse receiving & QC1 day
Total Lead Time34 days

With a 34-day lead time and an average daily demand of 50 units, this importer needs (34 × 50) = 1,700 units of lead time stock plus safety stock on top. Most importers incorrectly use only the 18-day freight time and then wonder why they stockout.

Why Lead Time Matters for Your Business

Accurate lead time is the foundation of all replenishment planning. Every other calculation — ROP, safety stock, EOQ — depends on getting lead time right.

  • Reorder point accuracy: ROP = Demand × Lead Time + Safety Stock. Wrong lead time = wrong ROP = stockouts
  • Safety stock sizing: Lead time variability directly determines how much safety stock you need
  • Supplier negotiation: Knowing your true total lead time helps you negotiate faster supplier processing or transit times

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I use average or maximum lead time for planning?▾
Use average lead time for your reorder point calculation, but account for lead time variability in your safety stock. The safety stock formula includes a service level factor that compensates for variability — the higher your lead time variability (standard deviation), the more safety stock you need. Using maximum lead time gives you very high safety stock that wastes capital.
How do I reduce supply chain lead time?▾
Key levers: (1) Negotiate faster supplier processing — many suppliers can expedite to 1 day vs. their standard 3-5 days for key customers, (2) Switch from sea to air freight for critical products, (3) Pre-position inventory closer to demand (regional warehouses), (4) Use a supplier in a closer geography, (5) Order more frequently in smaller quantities to reduce the planning horizon needed.
What is lead time variability and why does it matter?▾
Lead time variability is how much your actual lead time deviates from the average. If your average lead time is 21 days but sometimes it is 14 days and sometimes 35 days, you have high variability (standard deviation ~7 days). High variability requires more safety stock to maintain the same service level. Reducing variability (through reliable suppliers and consistent freight) is often more valuable than reducing average lead time.
