How a D2C brand cut dead stock and freed up working capital

Slow and dead stock quietly eats cash — cash you could spend on your bestsellers. Here's how to find it, put a number on what it's costing, and free it up.

ℹ️ Illustrative worked example — representative brand and numbers, not a real named customer. Validate against your own data before acting.

The situation

A D2C home & kitchen brand carries 600 SKUs. Growth has stalled and cash is tight, but the founder senses a lot of money is "sitting on shelves." The goal: find the non-moving SKUs, quantify the capital and holding cost they tie up, and turn that back into cash for fast movers before the next season.

Step 1 — Classify the catalogue (ABC)

Run ABC analysis: typically ~20% of SKUs drive ~80% of revenue (A items), while a long tail of C items barely sells. That long tail is where dead stock hides.

A items: ~120 SKUs → ~80% of revenue C items: ~300 SKUs → ~5% of revenue (dead-stock suspects)
📊 ABC Analysis Calculator
Step 2 — Identify and value the dead stock

Flag SKUs with zero or near-zero sales over the last 90+ days. Say 60 SKUs qualify, holding 8,000 units at an average cost of ₹250:

Dead stock value = 8,000 units × ₹250 = ₹20,00,000 (₹20L)
💀 Dead Stock Calculator
Step 3 — Add the carrying cost (the hidden bleed)

Dead stock doesn't just freeze cash — it costs ~20–25%/year to hold (storage, insurance, capital, obsolescence):

Annual carrying cost = ₹20L × 22% = ₹4.4L / year ≈ ₹37,000 every month it sits
💸 Carrying Cost Calculator
Step 4 — Act, then track turnover

Clear it deliberately: bundle slow C-items with bestsellers, run a clearance/EOSS push, liquidate the truly dead, and stop reordering them. Then watch inventory turnover climb as cash rotates into A-items.

✅ The outcome

By clearing even 70% of that dead stock, the brand frees roughly ₹14L of cash and stops ~₹26,000/month of carrying-cost bleed — capital it redeploys into A-items that actually sell. Inventory turnover rises, and reorder discipline stops the dead pile rebuilding.
